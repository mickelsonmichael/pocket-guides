Overview

Pokémon Yellow: Special Pikachu Edition is the third Generation I core-series game, based closely on the original anime. You receive Pikachu as your starter — it walks behind you, cannot be evolved or released, and reacts to your actions with expressions based on its happiness. The rival takes Eevee instead of a fire/water/grass starter.

Key differences from Red/Blue:
  • Starter is Pikachu only (rival gets Eevee — evolves based on your battle record)
  • Bulbasaur, Charmander, and Squirtle are obtainable as gifts mid-game
  • Pikachu follows you on the overworld and displays happiness expressions
  • Pokémon unavailable in one R/B version (Scyther, Pinsir, Oddish, Growlithe, etc.) are mostly all available in Yellow
  • Team Rocket's Pokémon (Ekans, Koffing, Meowth) are removed from the wild
  • Weedle, Kakuna, and Beedrill are removed; Caterpie is the only Bug in Viridian Forest
  • Farfetch'd and Lickitung now appear as wild Pokémon
  • Abra relocated to routes surrounding Saffron City
  • Gym leader teams and trainer rosters updated to match the anime
  • Charizard can now learn Fly via HM02
Pokémon not in Yellow: 13 Pokémon cannot be obtained in Yellow without trading from Red or Blue. See the Pokédex table for the full list (marked TRADE).

Starter Pokémon

Unlike Red/Blue, you don't choose a starter — you get Pikachu. However, all three original starters can be obtained as gifts later in the game, referencing the anime episodes where Ash recruits them.

#001 Bulbasaur

Cerulean City

A woman in the house to the west of the Cerulean City Gym will offer you Bulbasaur — but only if your Pikachu has high happiness. To check, open the status screen for your Pikachu; if it raises both arms and shows a big smile, you qualify. Keep Pikachu in your party, avoid letting it faint, and use Potions or vitamins on it to raise happiness quickly.

#004 Charmander

Route 24 (north of Cerulean, after Nugget Bridge)

A trainer named Damian left Charmander behind on a rock because he considered it weak. Talk to Charmander on the plateau above the bridge. Do not walk away without accepting it — once you leave the screen without taking it, Charmander will be gone permanently.

#007 Squirtle

Vermilion City

Officer Jenny will give you Squirtle in Vermilion City, but only after you defeat Lt. Surge (3rd Gym). Find Officer Jenny near the Pokémon Center / Gym area. Squirtle is the "problem Pokémon" that was causing trouble and she gives it to a qualified trainer.

Rival's Eevee evolution depends on your win/loss record in the first two rival battles:
Win both → Jolteon · Win at Oak's Lab, skip/lose Route 22 → Flareon · Lose at Oak's Lab → Vaporeon.
Your rival's team builds around that Eeveelution for the rest of the game.

Hidden Machines (HMs)

HMs can be used unlimited times. They cannot be discarded. HM moves cannot be deleted by a Pokémon (there is no Move Deleter in Gen I — be careful what you teach). A Gym Badge is required to use each HM outside of battle.

HM01 — Cut
Captain's cabin on the S.S. Anne (Vermilion City harbor). Heal the captain's seasickness to receive it. Cuts small trees blocking paths.
Requires: Cascade Badge (Misty, Cerulean Gym)
HM02 — Fly
Route 16 — enter the small house north of the Cycling Road gate (requires Cut to reach). A girl inside gives you Fly. Allows instant travel to any previously visited city.
Requires: Thunder Badge (Lt. Surge, Vermilion Gym)
HM03 — Surf
Safari Zone — reach the secret house at the far end of Area 3. The man inside gives you Surf as a reward for being the first to find it. Required to progress to Cinnabar Island and the Seafoam Islands.
Requires: Soul Badge (Koga, Fuchsia Gym)
HM04 — Strength
Fuchsia City — return the Safari Zone Warden's Gold Teeth (found in the Safari Zone, Area 3). He rewards you with Strength. Allows pushing heavy boulders, including the ones needed to access Articuno in Seafoam Islands.
Requires: Rainbow Badge (Erika, Celadon Gym)
HM05 — Flash
Route 2 eastern path — the building south of Diglett's Cave exit contains one of Professor Oak's aides. He gives you Flash if you have caught enough Pokémon. Flash lowers the opponent's Accuracy by 1 stage and is useful (but not required) for navigating the dark Rock Tunnel.
Requires: 10 Pokémon caught · No Badge required to use in battle

Stats System (Gen I)

Generation I uses a stat system significantly different from Generation III onwards. Understanding it lets you maximize your Pokémon's potential.

The Five Stats

In Gen I, there are only 5 stats: HP, Attack, Defense, Speed, and Special. There is no split between Special Attack and Special Defense — a single "Special" stat governs both how hard special moves hit AND how well you resist them. This makes Pokémon with high Special exceptionally powerful defensively and offensively.

Determinant Values (DVs)

What later games call IVs are called DVs (Determinant Values) in Gen I. Each Pokémon has a DV from 0 to 15 for Attack, Defense, Speed, and Special. The HP DV is derived from the other four:

HP_DV = (atk_DV & 1) << 3 | (def_DV & 1) << 2 | (spd_DV & 1) << 1 | (spc_DV & 1)
// Take the lowest bit of each DV in order: Atk, Def, Spd, Spc → form HP DV (0–15)

To get maximum HP DV (15), all four other DVs must be odd. DVs are fixed when a Pokémon is generated (caught, received as gift, etc.) and cannot be changed.

Wild Pokémon restriction: Pokémon encountered in tall grass, caves, or via Surf have limited DV pools due to the game's pseudorandom number generator — they are unlikely to have perfect DVs. Fishing encounters, gift Pokémon (Bulbasaur, Charmander, Squirtle, Lapras, Hitmonlee/Hitmonchan, Eevee), and static encounters (Snorlax, Mewtwo) can have any DV combination.

Stat Experience (Stat Exp)

This is the Gen I equivalent of EVs. Each stat has a Stat Exp value from 0 to 65,535. When you defeat a Pokémon, you gain Stat Exp equal to that species' base stat for each of the 5 stats.

// Stat calculation at level 100:
stat_bonus = floor( sqrt(StatExp) ) / 4 // rounded down (max bonus: 63)

HP = floor(( (Base + DV) × 2 + stat_bonus ) × Level / 100) + Level + 10
Other = floor(( (Base + DV) × 2 + stat_bonus ) × Level / 100) + 5

Key Differences from Modern EVs

  • No cap per stat and no total cap (unlike 510 EV cap in Gen III+). Stat Exp can reach 65,535 per stat
  • Every battle awards Stat Exp for all 5 stats simultaneously, based on the defeated Pokémon's full stat line
  • To fully max all stats, you would need to defeat ~656 Mew (each grants 100 Stat Exp to all stats) — practically impossible without grinding intentionally
  • Stats are not recalculated every level-up; they recalculate only on level-up OR when the Pokémon is stored in Bill's PC ("Box Trick")

The Box Trick

Box Trick: Storing a Pokémon in Bill's PC immediately recalculates its stats with all current Stat Exp factored in. This is useful if you've been gaining Stat Exp at Lv 100 (since level-up won't trigger recalculation at max level). Deposit into any Box, then withdraw — stats update instantly.

Vitamins

Vitamins add 2,560 to a single stat's Stat Exp, but cannot raise any stat's Stat Exp above 25,600. Use them early for a guaranteed boost, but battle-gained Stat Exp will exceed the vitamin cap naturally over time. Available at the Celadon Dept. Store (₽9,800 each):

  • HP Up — HP stat
  • Protein — Attack stat
  • Iron — Defense stat
  • Carbos — Speed stat
  • Calcium — Special stat

Optimizing Stats

  • Fight Pokémon that yield high base stats in your desired area (e.g., Chansey yields 250 base Special for Stat Exp)
  • Use the Box Trick at Lv 100 if you continue farming Stat Exp after hitting max level
  • DVs cannot be changed — to get a Pokémon with good DVs, you may need to soft-reset for gift Pokémon or trade-in Pokémon (which can have any DV set)
  • Wild Pokémon have restricted DVs due to the RNG — fishing and gift Pokémon are better candidates for high DVs
  • Rare Candy raises level without gaining Stat Exp — using too many leads to permanently weaker stats than battle-trained equivalents

Key Gen I Mechanics

The Special Stat (No Split)

In Gen I, there is no Special Attack / Special Defense split. The single Special stat serves both purposes. Pokémon like Chansey (base Special 105) are simultaneously great special attackers AND special walls. This was split into Sp. Atk and Sp. Def starting in Gen II.

Type Chart Quirks

The Gen I type chart has several bugs and differences from later games:

InteractionExpectedActual in Gen I
Ghost → Psychic2× super-effective0× — no effect (bug: Ghost-type moves do 0 damage to Psychic types)
Poison → Bug2× super-effective1× — normal damage (type chart bug; displays "super effective" in some cases)

The Ghost/Psychic immunity is famously exploitable: Psychic-type Pokémon (Alakazam, Starmie, Jynx) are effectively immune to Ghost-type attacks, making them dominant in competitive play.

Critical Hit Rate

In Gen I, critical hit rate is based on the user's base Speed stat divided by 512 (≈ Speed/512). Fast Pokémon have higher natural critical hit rates. Moves with high crit ratios (Slash, Razor Leaf, Crabhammer, Karate Chop) use Speed/64 instead — giving fast Pokémon like Persian and Electrode a ~23% crit rate with Slash.

Focus Energy bug: The move Focus Energy claims to double your critical hit rate, but due to a Gen I bug, it actually divides your critical hit rate by 4. Do not use Focus Energy — it makes things worse.

Badge Boosts

Earning Gym Badges permanently boosts certain stats for all of your Pokémon in battle. These multipliers stack and apply to the final computed stat value:

  • Boulder Badge (Brock) — Attack ×1.125
  • Cascade Badge (Misty) — Speed ×1.125
  • Thunder Badge (Lt. Surge) — Special ×1.125
  • Soul Badge (Koga) — Defense ×1.125
  • Earth Badge (Giovanni) — All stats ×1.125

By the time you reach the Elite Four, you have multiple badge boosts applied. The Earth Badge's all-stat boost makes the final stretch much more powerful.

Wrap / Bind / Fire Spin

In Gen I, multi-turn trapping moves (Wrap, Bind, Clamp, Fire Spin) completely prevent the trapped Pokémon from attacking for 2–5 turns while dealing chip damage. This was much stronger than in later games where the opponent can still attack. Wrapping strategies were extremely common in Gen I, especially with Dragonite.

Sleep

In Gen I, Sleep prevents action for 1–7 turns. Critically, the counter only decreases on the turn the Pokémon tries to wake (it must "fail" to act before waking). This makes sleep among the strongest status effects. Spore (Paras/Parasect) and Lovely Kiss (Jynx) are the highest-accuracy sleep moves.

One-Hit KO Moves

Horn Drill, Fissure, and Guillotine have a special accuracy mechanic in Gen I: if the user's Speed is higher than the target's Speed, the base accuracy is (user Speed - target Speed + 1). Against slower targets, OHKO moves can be nearly guaranteed. Against faster targets, they always miss. Fissure (TM27, from Giovanni) is particularly powerful in this regard.

Pikachu Happiness

Your starter Pikachu has a basic happiness/friendship system — the first such system in the series. Its mood affects the expressions it shows when you check its status. High happiness is required to receive Bulbasaur from the Cerulean City woman. To raise happiness: keep Pikachu in your party, avoid letting it faint, and use items on it. It will be unhappy if it faints frequently or if you rarely interact with it.

Safari Zone

The Safari Zone uses special rules: you cannot use your own Pokémon to battle. You have 30 Safari Balls and 500 steps. Options each turn: throw a Ball, throw Bait (makes Pokémon less likely to flee but harder to catch), throw a Rock (easier to catch but more likely to flee), or Run. Rare Pokémon like Tauros, Scyther, Pinsir, and Chansey are found here but can flee quickly — use Bait sparingly on rare ones.

Notable Items

Master Ball — Catches any Pokémon with 100% success. You get exactly one per game, from the Silph Co. president after rescuing him from Giovanni. Save it for Mewtwo (post-game) or use it on a legendary bird if you're having trouble catching it.
Rare Candy — Instantly raises a Pokémon's level by 1. Multiple are hidden throughout the game. Important caveat: Rare Candy does not grant Stat Exp. A Pokémon leveled via Rare Candy has lower stats than one leveled through battle. Use them sparingly or only at Lv 99→100 to avoid wasting Stat Exp gains.
Poké Flute — Given by Mr. Fuji in Lavender Town (rescue him from Team Rocket at the top of Pokémon Tower). Wakes sleeping Pokémon. Required to move the two Snorlax blocking Routes 12 and 16. Can also be used in battle to wake sleeping opponents — be careful.
Eevee — Free from the Celadon Mansion. You only get one, so choose your Eeveelution wisely: Jolteon (Electric, high Speed + Special), Vaporeon (Water, high HP + Special), Flareon (Fire, high Attack + Special). To get all three in one run, you need to trade additional Eevee from R/B.
Evolution Stones — Sold at the Celadon Dept. Store: Fire Stone (₽2,100), Water Stone (₽2,100), Thunder Stone (₽2,100), Leaf Stone (₽2,100). Moon Stones are found as hidden items (Mt. Moon, Route 2, Game Corner, Pokémon Mansion). You can buy unlimited stones at the store.
Silph Scope — Required to see and interact with the ghost Pokémon in Pokémon Tower. Obtained from Giovanni after defeating him in the Rocket Hideout beneath the Celadon Game Corner.
Bicycle — Obtained via a Bike Voucher from the Pokémon Fan Club in Vermilion City (talk to the chairman, listen to his story about his Pokémon, and he'll give it). Take the voucher to the Cerulean Bike Shop. Doubles your movement speed.
Fossils — You can only choose one fossil in Mt. Moon: Dome Fossil (Kabuto) or Helix Fossil (Omanyte). The Old Amber (Aerodactyl) is found separately in the Pewter Museum of Science back entrance (requires Cut). Take fossils to the Cinnabar Island Research Laboratory to resurrect them.
Surfing Pikachu (Pikachu's Beach): Route 19 has a Summer Beach House where you can play the Pikachu's Beach surfing mini-game — but only if your Pikachu knows Surf. In Yellow, the only way to teach Pikachu Surf is by connecting to Pokémon Stadium (the Japanese release) or by certain event distributions. This is essentially inaccessible in normal play.

Mew Guide

Mew (#151) is NOT legitimately obtainable in Pokémon Yellow. It was never distributed through normal gameplay and cannot be caught, found, or traded for through intended means. The only way to obtain a Mew in Yellow is through the "Mew Glitch" — an unintended exploit that works due to a programming error in all Generation I games.

The Mew Glitch (also called the Trainer-Fly glitch) works by exploiting the game's encounter-generation system. When you escape from a "long-range trainer" using Fly before they can initiate a battle, the game gets stuck in a state that generates a wild Pokémon encounter when you return — with the species determined by the Special stat of the last Pokémon you battled.

Mew's index number is 21, which matches the Special stat of a level-17 Slowpoke — conveniently found on a Youngster on Route 25.

Method #1 (Classic Route)

Prerequisites: Must have Fly (HM02) and a Pokémon that knows it. Neither the Gambler on Route 8 nor the Youngster on Route 25 should have been defeated yet.

  • Travel to Route 8. Stand directly beneath the Underground Path entrance (the door going south into the tunnel). Save your game here.
  • Take one step south. As you step, immediately hold or press Start to open the menu. The Gambler NPC (facing north toward the path) will scroll onto screen — his line of sight will register you for one frame before you open the menu.
  • From the Start menu, use Fly and fly to Cerulean City. The Gambler's exclamation mark will appear but the Fly animation takes over. The game now believes you're in a mid-battle state.
  • In Cerulean City, your Start/A/B buttons behave strangely. Walk north to Route 25 and find the Youngster facing north with a level-17 Slowpoke. Let him walk up and initiate battle normally. Defeat his Slowpoke (Special stat = 21).
  • After the battle, Fly or walk back to Route 8. When you enter the area where the Gambler was, the Start menu will pop up on its own. Close it.
  • A wild Pokémon encounter starts immediately. It is Mew at level 7. Use your best catch strategy (put it to sleep, whittle HP) and throw Poké Balls.
Important: Mew obtained via glitch has OT data that does not match the official Nintendo distribution (OT: "GF", Trainer ID: 22796). As a result, if using the Virtual Console version of Yellow, Glitch Mew cannot be transferred via Poké Transporter to Pokémon Bank or modern games — it will be flagged as invalid. Only the officially distributed Mew (or a Mew obtained via arbitrary code execution to match the exact OT/ID) can be transferred.
Alternative Method: The Swimmer on Route 24 (facing east, with a level-16 Shellder — Special stat 21) can replace the Route 25 Youngster. The process is identical but uses a different trainer and location for the "reset" battle. The same Mew (Lv 7) appears.
